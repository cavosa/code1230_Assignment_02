Unpaid Child Care by Age

Background

Data journalism is a journalism specialty reflecting the increased role that numerical data is used in the production and distribution of information in the digital era. It reflects the increased interaction between content producers (journalist) and several other fields such as design, computer science and statistics

The data that will be presented in this data journal shows the Count of persons aged 15 years and over who in the two weeks prior to census night, spent time caring for a child/children (under 15 years) without pay.

Statistical Areas Level 1

Mesh blocks are built wholly to represent geographical areas or Statistical Areas Level 1 (SA1). Census data has been designed and released in the smallest unit by SA1s, which have a population of between 200 and 800 people with an average population size of 400 people. The census data itself is collected every 5 years.

Dataset: SA1 - G22a Unpaid Child Care by age by Sex

SA1 based data for Unpaid Child Care by Age by Sex, in General Community Profile (GCP), 2016 Census. G22 is broken up into two sections (G22a - G22b), this section contains 'Females 15-19 years Cared for other child/children only' - 'Persons 85 years and over Cared for Total'.

Story Behind the Data

The data shown here was collected from participatory individuals. However, a much broader demographic has been shunned from coverage. Taking care of children, in general, is working. It might be unpaid, but it is still work and, if parents didn’t do it, they would have to pay someone else to do it. Hence, unpaid childcare is Australia’s largest industry.

After pre-analysis, the data presented in this journal aims to show updated statistics of involvement in unpaid child care by gender, across the more popular age groups among child care participants. Moreover, it identifies their apparent roles in society, like in this case, those that are in the early retirement age participate the most in upaid child care. This may point to the fact that young parents often leave their children with the grandparents to take care of.
